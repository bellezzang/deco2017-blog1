The brief asks us to design a community hub for people who share a particular interest and can communicate both through **experience and information exchange**. My interpretation of the brief is that the project should avoid turning into generic social media platform. A successful community hub needs to understand what the community really cares about, what problems they repeatedly face, and what kind of interaction will engage them back. This means the main design challenge is not simply to add common features such as posts, comments, or profiles, but to define a clear purpose for the community.

The A2 overview also makes it clear that the prototype should focus on the unique feature that differentiates the community from platforms such as Reddit or Facebook. Since standard functions such as login and authentication are already provided, our group is expected to use the prototype to show why this community would benefit from a more tailored experience. and not recreate the same features that already exist.

In accordance with this interpretation, our group decided to develope **Bakelab – a community hub for serious sourdough bakers**. From our discovery research, it became evident that this community is quite different from general recipe users. Besides recipes, serious sourdough bakers are not only looking for instructions. They care about **process, repetition, and improvement**. They want to understand why one loaf succeeds and another fails, and how variables such as hydration, starter activity, fermentation time, temperature, and folding affect the final result.

This helped us identify that the shared information in BakeLab should not be casual food photos or recipe posts alone. The more valuable information is **structured baking context**: what the baker changed, what conditions they used, what result they got, and what problem they are trying to solve. In this sense, the hub should support learning through process, not just sharing finished outcomes.

A key problem from our research is that sourdough bakers often use fragmented tools. They may track notes in one place, use calculators somewhere else, manage starter reminders separately, and then ask for advice on social platforms. This makes it difficult to connect the full baking process with the final outcome. It also weakens community feedback because advice is less useful when a post lacks context.

Because of this, our early product direction is not a recipe app, but a community-driven baking lab. The main value of BakeLab should be helping users **record, review, and discuss bakes** in a more structured way. Instead of designing around passive scrolling, the hub should support **diagnosis, comparison, and shared learning**.
